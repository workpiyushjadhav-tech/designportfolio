The Problem

Group dining in food halls is genuinely chaotic.

Split queues, food arriving in waves, one lump sum split eight ways equally — even when two people didn't drink and one person only had a salad.

Picture this: your group of eight friends picks a food hall for dinner. Everyone's excited. Then reality kicks in. You're split across four different queues. The person who got the wood-fired pizza is still waiting 20 minutes later while everyone else's food is going cold. Then comes the bill — one lump sum, split eight ways equally.

Nobody says anything. But someone's frustrated, someone's quietly doing maths in their head, and the person who organised it all has already braced themselves for the inevitable "hey can you send me your details" messages that'll drag on until Wednesday.

The product that should exist: a QR-triggered web app where one person opens a "Host" session, everyone joins on their own phone, browses a single unified menu across every vendor in the hall, places their order, and settles their own share in one go — no app download, no account creation, no post-dinner payment chasing.

That product didn't exist. So we designed it.

Research & Discovery

Five evidence areas. One structural gap.

Secondary research structured across food hall culture, group dining behaviour, QR adoption, real-time tracking expectations, and payment splitting psychology.

Evidence Area 01

Food Hall Culture & Growth

UK food halls grew from 76 to 84 operational venues between June 2023 and February 2024 — a 15% increase in eight months — with an additional 18% increase in venues under development.

  • 65 further venues in development nationally
  • Average £5.6M annual revenue per site, 10%+ YoY growth
  • 76% of consumers seek venues combining dining + entertainment
  • Format is structurally a group dining proposition — yet technology hasn't caught up
🏢 Insert: UK Food Hall Growth Visual
Evidence Area 02

Group Dining Behaviour

Behavioural economics research reveals the hidden cost of dining in groups.

  • 37% more spending when splitting equally (Gneezy, Haruvy & Yafe, 2004)
  • Sequential ordering creates conformity pressure — first orderer sets unspoken spending ceiling
  • 62% of diners report higher anxiety splitting bills with acquaintances than close friends
  • 78% prefer paying only for what they ordered — but social cost prevents it
🧠 Insert: Behavioural Research Visual
Evidence Area 03

QR Ordering Adoption

QR ordering has moved from trend to standard across UK hospitality.

  • 75% of restaurants globally use QR codes; scan volumes up 400% in two years
  • 58% of UK consumers scanned QR in bar/restaurant; 80% agree QR makes life easier
  • 92% of UK population owns a smartphone with native QR scanning — zero barrier to entry
  • QR systems reduced wait times 25% in a UK cafe chain (QRCode.co.uk, 2025)
📱 Insert: QR Adoption Visual
Evidence Area 04

Existing Food Hall QR Audit

A review of 9 UK food halls found that only 4 had any QR ordering capability — and of those, none supported multi-vendor group sessions.

  • Mackie Mayor + Altrincham Market House: me&u (QR, multi-vendor)
  • Boxpark: QR for drinks only
  • Arcade Food Hall, Market Halls, STACK, Mercato Metropolitano: counter service only
  • The gap is structural, not incidental
🗺️ Insert: Food Hall Audit Visual
Evidence Area 05

Payment Splitting Psychology

Money conversations at dinner carry more social risk than most people realise.

  • 72% find discussing finances harder than death, religion, or politics (Splitty, 2025)
  • Simultaneously managing mental arithmetic + social impression creates genuine stress
  • 3.2× more likely to overpay than risk perceived conflict
  • The desire for fairness exists — the social architecture for it doesn't

Competitive Landscape

What competitors get right — and what they miss.

Analysis across me&u, Uber Eats, Just Eat, and Revel Systems to identify design opportunities.

Strengths (Competitors)
  • me&u enables table ordering without a dedicated app download
  • Uber Eats has best-in-class live order tracking UX
  • Just Eat has well-tested dietary filter patterns
  • Existing QR ordering reduces perceived wait time by 25%
Weaknesses (Competitors)
  • No multi-vendor group session without pre-setup
  • Account creation required by all major platforms
  • Payment is always post-meal or host-led — never individual at order time
  • Dietary filtering is per-vendor, never cross-hall
Opportunities (Culinarr)
  • Guest checkout — no account, no friction at entry point
  • Cross-vendor dietary filter at session level
  • Transparent itemised payment as the default, not toggle
  • Group session via QR scan — zero pre-planning by host
Risks & Threats
  • me&u could add group session to existing product
  • Noisy food hall environment tests usability harder than lab
  • Low digital literacy in older diner segment not tested
  • Venue operators may be slow to adopt new ordering platform

Key Design Decision

The payment split conflict: four options, one right answer.

Person A ordered generously across multiple vendors. Person B ordered only a bowl of ramen. This tension is not a niche edge case.

Rejected
Host-controlled split
Host sets split mode for everyone before ordering. Disproportionate power to host; latecomers have no say.
Rejected
Democratic group vote
Majority choice applied to whole table. Adds friction before anyone has seen the menu.
Rejected
Hybrid split
Personal items individual; shared plates split among selected participants. Too complex to design and build within scope.
Selected
Transparent itemised default
Everyone defaults to paying their own items — the app already knows who ordered what. Equal split surfaced as a toggle, not the default.
MoSCoW Priorities

Mapping what must exist before anything else.

Visual Architecture & Design

One layout system. Every menu.

The most visually complex challenge was making a burger menu, a sushi menu, and a cocktail menu feel like they belong in the same product without looking like a generic aggregator.

The Dual User Journey
Scenario A
Everyone arrives together
Host scans QR → creates session → app generates 4-digit code + QR → friends scan host's screen → shared session → each person browses and adds → host reviews group basket → submits → each person pays their own items.
Scenario B
Someone arrives late
Host scans QR → creates session → shares link via WhatsApp/iMessage → late arrivals tap link → land directly in group session → add items before or after arriving → host submits when everyone is ready.

Guests have full autonomy over what they order but zero control over submission timing. That stays with the host. This prevents accidental early submission while letting everyone browse and add at their own pace.

Key Screens — 4 Core Flows
Flow 01

Host: Create & Share Group Session

One tap to create a session after QR scan. The host gets a 4-digit room code, a scannable QR, and a shareable link — whichever works for the group's situation.

  • No account creation — guest checkout from the start
  • Auto table number detection from QR metadata
  • Session closes when host submits — guests can't accidentally submit
📱 Insert: Host Session Screen
Flow 02

Browse: Unified Multi-Vendor Menu

One component system across every vendor. Variety comes from photos and the vendor's colour header strip — the structure stays identical.

  • Cross-vendor dietary filter — not per-vendor silo
  • Dietary tags include text labels alongside icons (WCAG 1.4.1)
  • OpenDyslexic font mode for users with dyslexia (Zara persona)
  • Estimated prep time shown before ordering
🍽️ Insert: Unified Menu Screen
Flow 03

Payment: Transparent Itemised Split

The app defaults everyone to paying for their own items — it already knows who ordered what. Equal split is a toggle, not the default.

  • Real-time "remaining table balance" shows how much of the group total has been settled
  • Apple Pay / Google Pay supported
  • One person's failed payment never affects another's confirmed order
  • "Need help?" button alerts venue staff — shifts responsibility from host to operator
💳 Insert: Payment Split Screen
Flow 04

Track: Live Order Status Per Vendor

Live order status per item, per vendor — the strongest differentiator and the most-regretted skip in the Buy a Feature session.

  • Each item tracked: Received → Preparing → Ready
  • Notification when all group items are ready
  • Scored highest in Q5 (functions well integrated) on SUS
  • Absent from every existing competitor in this context

Design Iteration

What testing changed.

01
Dietary filter discoverability — failure in 2 of 5 participants (P3, P5)
  • Issue Dietary filter icon was mistaken for a general settings control by both failing participants. P3 spent 40 seconds looking before asking.
  • Fix Filter relocated to a persistent top-bar position alongside search, with a text label "Filter by diet" replacing the icon-only treatment. Both align with WCAG 1.4.1.

Reflection

The journey map was where the real insight lived.

What I'd do differently: Recruit actual food hall users. All five participants were university peers with high digital literacy. The real audience includes older diners, people less confident with technology, non-English speakers ordering in unfamiliar cuisines, and people in genuinely noisy group settings. An in-situ pilot at Mackie Mayor or Altrincham Market House would have surfaced friction that a lab session can't.

What this taught me about process: I started the project assuming the payment friction was the core problem. The journey map showed the emotional trough was deepest at the ordering stage — when the host is guessing for six people in WhatsApp while four have responded and two haven't. Solving coordination first, payment second, changed the entire product priority order.

The Buy a Feature surprise: I expected Feature 14 (live order tracking) to dominate, and it did — but Feature 9 (join group session) scoring 0/3 was the result I hadn't anticipated. It forced a reassessment of an assumption I'd treated as settled and made the final join flow significantly more minimal.

Open Questions
?Current prototype supports a single order round — second round of drinks mid-session is architecturally supported but out of scope for this iteration.
?Accessibility testing not conducted with assistive technology users despite dyslexia being a primary design driver.
?The experience under real food hall conditions — noise, poor signal, time pressure, group social dynamics — remains untested.
Business Metrics (Post-Launch)
→ Table turnover rate: Did groups spend less time ordering and paying? Benchmark: me&u's 30% AOV increase.
→ Avg ticket size per head: Deloitte's ~20% spending increase with digital tableside ordering sets the baseline expectation.
→ Cart abandonment rate: Tracking abandonment by screen and split method would reveal exactly what to fix next.
What This Points To

Three things this design proves are possible.

🔗
Group session without friction
A group ordering session that works without an app, an account, or a single designated payer.
🍜
Unified multi-vendor menu
A menu that stays coherent and accessible regardless of cuisine type, across every vendor in the hall.
💳
Payment with genuine flexibility
A payment flow presented before anyone pays anything — not as a last-minute complication.

UK food halls grew 31% in a single year. 65 more are in development. The format is inherently a group dining proposition. But every piece of technology deployed inside these venues was built for solo diners or single-restaurant contexts. None of these are technically impossible. They just haven't been designed for this context before.
