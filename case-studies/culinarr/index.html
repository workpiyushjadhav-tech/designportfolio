The Problem

Group dining in food halls is genuinely chaotic.

Split queues, food arriving in waves, one lump sum split eight ways equally — even when two people didn't drink and one only had a salad. Nobody designed technology for this.

The product that should exist: a QR-triggered web app where one person opens a session, everyone joins on their own phone, browses across every vendor, and settles their own share. No app download, no account, no post-dinner payment chasing.

That product didn't exist. So we designed it.

Research & Discovery

Five evidence areas. One structural gap.

Secondary research, competitive audit of 9 UK food halls, review mining across TripAdvisor, Reddit, Google, and Facebook. Zero competitors support multi-vendor group sessions without pre-setup.

Evidence 01

Group Dining Behaviour

Behavioural economics reveals a hidden cost to dining in groups that the current technology does nothing to address.

  • 37% more spending when splitting equally (Gneezy, Haruvy & Yafe, 2004)
  • Sequential ordering creates conformity pressure — first orderer sets unspoken spending ceiling
  • 62% report higher anxiety splitting with acquaintances vs close friends
  • 78% prefer paying only for what they ordered — but social cost prevents asking
🧠Insert: Behavioural Research Visual
Evidence 02

UK Food Hall QR Audit — The Gap

A review of 9 UK food halls found only 4 had any QR ordering capability. Of those, none supported multi-vendor group sessions.

  • Mackie Mayor + Altrincham Market House: me&u (single-vendor group)
  • Boxpark: QR for drinks only
  • Arcade Food Hall, Market Halls, STACK: counter service only
  • The gap is structural, not incidental — no product exists for this context
🗺️Insert: Food Hall Audit Visual
Evidence 03

Payment Splitting Psychology

The money conversation at dinner carries more social risk than most people realise — and more than most products account for.

  • 72% find discussing finances harder than death, religion, or politics (Splitty, 2025)
  • 3.2× more likely to overpay than risk perceived conflict
  • Simultaneously managing mental arithmetic + social impression creates genuine stress
  • The desire for fairness exists — the social architecture for it doesn't

From Research to Direction

Research showed coordination was the real problem. Payment was the symptom.

The journey map emotion arc made this visible — anxiety peaks at ordering, not checkout. That changed where the product needed to focus.

How Might We

How might we help groups coordinate orders, pay fairly, and enjoy the social experience — without any app, account, or designated payer?

Key Design Decision

The payment split: four options, one right answer.

Buy a Feature (3 participants, £100 each, £140 total feature cost) validated priorities — and challenged one assumption. Feature 9 (Join session) scored 0/3, directly contradicting its Must Have classification. The join flow became significantly more minimal as a result.

Rejected
Host-controlled split
Host sets split mode for everyone before ordering. Disproportionate power; latecomers have no say.
Rejected
Democratic group vote
Majority choice applied to whole table. Adds friction before anyone has seen the menu.
Rejected
Hybrid split
Personal items individual; shared plates split among selected participants. Too complex within scope.
Selected
Transparent itemised default
Everyone defaults to paying their own items — the app already knows who ordered what. Equal split is a toggle, not the default.
Solution

One layout system. Every menu.

The most visually complex challenge: making a burger menu, a sushi menu, and a cocktail menu feel coherent without looking like a generic aggregator.

The Dual User Journey
Scenario A
Everyone arrives together
Host scans QR → creates session → 4-digit code + QR generated → friends scan → each person browses and adds → host submits → each person pays their own items.
Scenario B
Someone arrives late
Host creates session → shares link via WhatsApp → late arrival taps link → lands directly in session → adds items → host submits when everyone is ready.

Guests have full autonomy over what they order but zero control over submission timing. That stays with the host — preventing accidental early submission while everyone browses at their own pace.

Key Screens — 4 Core Flows
Flow 01

Host: Create & Share Group Session

One tap to create a session after QR scan. 4-digit code, scannable QR, and shareable link — whichever works for the group.

  • No account creation — guest checkout from the start
  • Auto table number detection from QR metadata
  • Session closes when host submits — guests can't accidentally submit
📱Insert: Host Session Screen
Flow 02

Browse: Unified Multi-Vendor Menu

One component system across every vendor. Variety comes from photos and vendor colour headers — structure stays identical.

  • Cross-vendor dietary filter — not per-vendor silo
  • Dietary tags include text labels alongside icons (WCAG 1.4.1)
  • OpenDyslexic font mode (Zara persona)
🍽️Insert: Unified Menu Screen
Flow 03

Payment: Transparent Itemised Split

Defaults everyone to paying their own items. Equal split is a toggle, not the default.

  • Real-time "remaining table balance" shows group total settled
  • Apple Pay / Google Pay supported
  • One person's failed payment never affects another's confirmed order
💳Insert: Payment Split Screen
Flow 04

Track: Live Order Status Per Vendor

Live tracking per item, per vendor. Absent from every existing competitor in this context.

  • Each item tracked: Received → Preparing → Ready
  • Notification when all group items are ready
  • Scored highest in Q5 (functions well integrated) on SUS

Reflection

What worked, what didn't.

What Worked
✓The journey map reframe. I started the project assuming payment friction was the core problem. The emotion arc showed anxiety peaks at ordering — when the host is guessing for six people in WhatsApp. Solving coordination first changed the entire product priority order.
✓Submission control staying with the host. Clean separation of concerns — guests control what they order, host controls when it's submitted. Solved a real failure mode without adding friction to the browsing experience.
✓One card component for every cuisine. Variety comes from photos and vendor colour headers. The structure stays identical — coherent without looking like a generic aggregator.
What Didn't / What's Unresolved
✕Dietary filter discoverability. Two of five participants failed this task entirely. A pure IA problem — the filter needed earlier placement, not better iconography.
✕Buy a Feature with only 3 participants. Feature 9 scoring 0/3 was compelling — but with three people, it's genuinely hard to know whether that's signal or sample noise. Needed six to eight participants to be defensible.
?Untested in real conditions. All five participants were university peers with high digital literacy. The real audience includes older diners, non-English speakers, people in genuinely noisy food hall environments. An in-situ pilot would surface friction the lab can't.
