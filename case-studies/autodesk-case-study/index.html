Two workflows. One platform.No way to connect them.

A 6-week industry project with Autodesk. The problem space was new territory: large-scale construction coordination using BIM software. We got up to speed quickly, defined a clear scope from a live brief, and designed a platform-level solution connecting two workflows that users needed together but the product kept apart.

The Problem

Two forms of analysis. Completely separate places.

Autodesk Construction Cloud has Change Analysis and Clash Analysis, both critical to BIM workflows. In the application, they are hosted in entirely different parts of the platform. In users' real work, they are inseparable.

Change Analysis
What was added, removed, or modified

Tracks modifications made to 3D building model objects across versions. Essential for understanding what moved, who moved it, and when.

Clash Analysis
Where objects overlap in space

Identifies when model objects physically overlap, conflicts that must be resolved before a building can be constructed. Every clash has a cause. That cause is almost always a change.

When a clash is found, users need to trace it back to the change that introduced it. To resolve a clash, users need to record the change made and verify it did not introduce new problems. Neither workflow currently supports the other.

Research & Discovery

Large-scale analysis. Two themes rose above everything else.

Three research phases across a 6-week Agile sprint — Discovery, Ideation, Evaluation — working directly with Autodesk's worldwide experts and customer base.

01
Heuristic Evaluation + Large-Scale Content Analysis

Nielsen's 10 heuristics applied to existing ACC workflows. Alongside this, over 25,000 comments from Reddit, YouTube, and Autodesk forums were collected and filtered using AI-assisted coding.

  • AI assisted in deciding which data to code. All codes and themes were human-defined.
  • 160 actionable insights generated before thematic synthesis
  • Key finding: Users struggled to connect what changed in the model with what broke. The interface gave them too little feedback and too much friction when moving between the two workflows.
02
Thematic Analysis - Five Themes, Two Dominant

Codes were grouped into five overarching themes. Two rose significantly above the others in frequency and impact.

  • Positives
  • Pain Points and Frustrations
  • Workflow Challenges - dominant theme
  • Visibility Gaps - dominant theme
  • Manual Workarounds
03
Benchmark Analysis + Analogous Inspiration

Competitor platforms investigated directly and indirectly related to the problem space. A key finding came from a market-leading platform in the same space.

  • A leading platform in this space tracks clashes through a clear status progression: New → Active → Resolved
  • This gives teams a shared view of what has been dealt with and what still needs attention
  • The platform we were studying had no equivalent. Clashes were detected but never formally tracked to resolution. That gap became a direct design target.
  • Analogous Inspiration surfaced useful patterns from outside the BIM field

The problem was not that ACC had too little data. It had too much, siloed in the wrong places. The solution was not to add more. It was to create continuity between what already existed.

From Research to Direction

Workflow Challenges and Visibility Gaps converged on one core problem.

HMW questions generated from thematic analysis, prioritised using an impact × frequency matrix, then formalised into a MoSCoW requirements set.

Problem Statement

When something breaks in a construction model, teams have no structured way to find out what caused it. Changes and clashes live in separate places. Connecting them is manual, slow, and easy to get wrong.

Key HMW Directions
Connect clash to change
HMW trace a detected clash back to the specific change that introduced it, in a single workflow rather than across two separate tools?
Reduce cognitive overhead
HMW reduce the effort of navigating large volumes of clash data without sufficient contextual control?
Proactive over reactive
HMW move teams from a reactive stance, discovering clashes after they occur, to a proactive one?
Create an audit trail
HMW record how clashes were resolved and surface that history when similar conflicts arise in future model iterations?
Solution

Two contributions. One unified principle.

The final prototype emerged from iterative wireframing across three Agile sprints, shaped by BIM community research, stakeholder engagement sessions, and mentor critique at each sprint gate.

01
Object / Filter-Based Approach to Clash & Change Analysis

One of the core friction points identified during research was cognitive overhead: navigating large volumes of clash data without sufficient contextual control. Users were forced to manually sift through dense lists.

The proposed solution introduced an object and filter-based interaction model, allowing users to isolate, group, and prioritise clashes based on relevant parameters such as discipline, severity, or model element type. This gave project stakeholders greater agency over what they were reviewing, reducing noise and enabling more focused decision-making during clash review sessions.

02
Predictive Clash Forecasting

The second contribution addressed a more forward-looking need: moving teams from reactive to proactive clash management.

Rather than only surfacing clashes after they occur, the prototype explored a predictive forecasting model that could signal likely conflict zones based on patterns in model changes over time. This was directly informed by the thematic finding that late-stage clash discovery was a significant source of project delays and rework in BIM professional workflows.

Evaluation

The research direction is well-grounded. The prototype needs real-world testing.

Three limitations identified, named honestly, each pointing to a concrete next step.

What the Research Validates
✓
Problem direction — Workflow Challenges and Visibility Gaps were dominant across all five themes, consistent across 25,000+ comments from multiple independent sources.
✓
Benchmark pattern — A Clash Status Lifecycle (New → Active → Resolved) is a tested, deployed model that confirms the design direction has precedent in the market.
✓
Stakeholder alignment — Expert interviews with Autodesk contacts were used to cross-reference publicly available data and validate the problem framing.
✓
Stakeholder reception — The prototype and research findings were presented to Autodesk contacts at the end of the sprint. The predictive forecasting direction stood out as the most ambitious contribution, taking the brief beyond simple integration toward a genuinely different way of working. Feedback at each sprint gate was used to refine decisions before moving forward.
⚠
End-user testing — Not conducted. The prototype was not tested with real BIM Managers or Engineers. Required before any deployment decision.
01
Data collection scope
  • GapPublic datasets skew toward frustrated users. Power users, satisfied users, and those who simply get on with the tool are largely invisible. Findings are directionally valid but need validation against a broader sample.
  • Next StepStructured research with a representative cross-section of ACC users across disciplines and project types.
02
No end-user testing
  • GapThe prototype was not tested with real BIM Managers or Engineers. Given the domain complexity — high-stakes construction workflows — this is a significant gap that stakeholder interviews cannot substitute for.
  • Next StepTask-based usability study with BIM Managers and Engineers using the integrated prototype in a real project context.
03
Generalisability of platform findings
  • GapFindings from the platform audit may not generalise beyond the specific workflows studied. ACC serves a wide range of construction disciplines with different coordination priorities.
  • Next StepValidation of the predictive forecasting model against historical clash and change data from live ACC projects across multiple disciplines.
Reflection

What worked, what didn't.

What Worked
AI-assisted filtering at scale. Processing 25,000+ comments manually would have taken weeks. Using AI to filter into 160 actionable insights before human thematic coding handled a data volume no small team could manage line by line in 6 weeks, while keeping all codes and themes human-defined.
Predictive forecasting going beyond the brief. The original challenge asked how to integrate two existing views. Predictive forecasting moved the answer from better integration to a fundamentally different mental model — proactive rather than reactive. The research supported the ambition.
Benchmark as a design anchor. Rather than designing a resolution tracking system from scratch, identifying that a market-leading competitor already used a tested status model gave us a concrete gap to articulate and a pattern to build from.
What Didn't / What's Unresolved
No end-user testing. The prototype was never tested with a real BIM Manager or Engineer on a real project. For a domain this technically specific, that's the gap that matters most. Stakeholder interviews helped but they don't replace it.
Public data as a proxy. Reddit and YouTube comments surface frustration loudly. They don't surface satisfaction or expertise. The findings needed validation against a broader, more representative user base.
Role-based view logic unresolved. BIM Managers, Engineers, and Architects have different needs when reviewing clashes and changes. The prototype addressed integration but the permission model and role-based view logic would require a further sprint to resolve properly.

This project required operating at two levels simultaneously: workflow design (when and where to surface data, and to which users) and UI design (how two datasets interact in the interface). Most UX projects are one or the other. This was both.
