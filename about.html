The Origin

Before the pixels, there were people.

For nearly a decade I worked in social services — sitting with people in crisis, building trust under pressure, and finding the right words when there weren't any easy answers. That wasn't a detour from design. It was the training ground.

I learned to listen deeper than the stated problem, to hold space for ambiguity, and to make decisions with incomplete information. Those skills don't show up in a resume objective, but they show up in every design decision I make.

The Pivot

Finding design by way of empathy.

The transition to UX wasn't a career pivot so much as a realization: the work I was already doing — understanding people's mental models, removing barriers, designing better experiences for the most vulnerable — that was design.

I started building. First side projects. Then a portfolio. Then, one job title change later, a new chapter.

How I Work

Principles I design by

Not a manifesto — just the things I catch myself saying in every design review.

🔍
Curiosity over assumption
I ask "why?" one more time than feels comfortable. The answer is almost always different from what I expected, and almost always more useful.
⚡
Speed in low fidelity
Expensive ideas fail cheap when you draw them on paper first. I move fast with sketches and slow down only when the direction is earned.
🤝
Design is a team sport
The best outcomes come from pulling engineers, PMs, and stakeholders into the process early — not presenting polished work to an audience.
♿
Accessibility is craft
Designing for the edges makes the center stronger. Accessible design is better design — for everyone, not just users with disabilities.
📐
Pixel-level intentionality
The 2px that separates "polished" from "almost there" matters. Craft and strategy aren't in tension — great execution amplifies great thinking.
🌱
Stay a beginner
The designers I admire most are voracious learners. Expertise is earned, but so is stagnation — I stay uncomfortable on purpose.
